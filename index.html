Using benchmarks for design

  • Benchmarks are historical medians from real programmes — not targets, standards, or predictions of future performance.
  • Use them to check whether a design is typical or an outlier, not to judge it “right” or “wrong”.
  • Reported results often fall short of targets (see the reality-check) — design to expected actuals, not nominal targets.
  • Don’t use them as strict targets or for accountability — they describe the past, under a specific (IATI-only) sample.

#read_me

Method, provenance and caveats. Source: IATI Standard via the Code for IATI datastore · developing countries · pulled 2026-06-02.

What this is

A reference set of real comparable programmes and live benchmarks to inform programme design — realistic scope, budget, duration and targets. It is a design reference, not an M&E tracking system, and not a scoring or ranking tool: nothing here rates an organisation or programme as “better”.

What each view answers

The tabs map to the questions asked when designing a programme — discovery first, comparison second, method always visible.

  • Programmes — what comparable work exists? The filterable evidence base, by country, sector, donor and status.
  • Benchmarks — what is typical? Median budget, duration and results-reporting rate by peer group. A reference, not a ranking.
  • Charts — what is the shape of the sample? Budget distribution, time trend, scale vs duration, regional split.
  • Country profiles — who funds what, where? Spend by sector and funder, indicators per sector, top providing countries.
  • Plan a programme — is my design realistic? Build a like-with-like cohort, then place your budget, duration and reach against it, with a reality-check on reported actuals.
  • Reported outcomes — how reliable is the results evidence? Indicator baseline→target→actual, achieved shown per row and never aggregated.
  • #read_me — how was every figure derived? Provenance, formulas, caveats and coverage.

Scope

All developing countries — World Bank non–high-income economies (high-income and aggregates excluded); 124 countries appear here. A programme is kept if it started on or after 2021-06-02 (the last 5 years) or is currently ongoing/planned (IATI Implementation or Pipeline), so recently-closed programmes are included. Each programme's country is its primary recipient (highest reported share); multi-country programmes are marked “+”.

Provenance — every figure is one of

REPORTED — straight from the programme's own IATI record (budget, dates, reach, baseline/target/actual). Real.
DERIVED — computed from reported figures: duration from dates, ≈USD via FX, real (constant-2024) USD via US CPI, achieved share = actual/target.
ESTIMATE — an educated value anchored on comparable reporters, always tagged. This dataset ships none: IATI's reach and target/actual fields proved too inconsistent to anchor a reliable cost or achievement estimate.
GAP — no defensible basis, so left blank — never guessed (see cost-per-beneficiary).
REFERENCE — external standards and evidence (not from this dataset), used for the sector design guidance in the planner. Each point is attributed to a named, linked source (e.g. Sphere, WHO/DCP3, GEEAP, PEFA, IFAD/FAO, ESMAP, ALNAP, OECD-DAC) and flagged indicative — validate locally; not a target.

Key caveats

  1. Cost per beneficiary is deliberately not computed. IATI reach indicators are non-comparable — they mix direct beneficiaries, indirect/population reach (one activity claimed 30,000,000 reached) and non-people counts (hectares, research products). Dividing budget by these is meaningless; raw reach is kept with its indicator label so you can judge each. For unit costs use sector studies, Sphere and cluster guidance.
  2. Targets vs actuals. Where both are reported, actuals frequently fall well short (e.g. 80 of 600 households). Design to expected actuals, not nominal targets.
  3. Double counting. The same activity can be reported by funder, recipient and implementer under different IDs. Deduped by IATI identifier, but residual funding-chain overlap remains.
  4. Coverage. Multilaterals are well represented globally, but programmes reported above country level (no single recipient country) are excluded by the developing-country focus, and a few large multilateral facilities publish only at aggregate level. Single records whose budget exceeds $2bn are almost always such facility/portfolio totals (e.g. multi-donor trust funds) rather than discrete programmes; they are tagged “facility” in the grid and held out of the charts so they don’t distort the scale. For country macro data use the World Bank API.
  5. Sample & medians. Each sector embeds a draw from the larger recent universe shown below — not ranked by budget, so budget medians approximate the population rather than skewing high. Thinly-published sectors are topped up from the official IATI Datastore by a publisher-capped draw: each sector takes the most recent activities across donors with at most ~6 per publisher (round-robin), so no single donor dominates a sector’s median. Where few donors publish recent activity in a purpose code, that sector is left small rather than padded with one publisher — so per-sector n varies (see the calibration table’s “In sample” column). It remains an IATI-only sample, so treat medians as indicative, not a census; for the authoritative full population use the OECD CRS or the complete IATI Datastore.
  6. FX is indicative. ≈USD uses fixed rates (below), not date-matched — order-of-magnitude only. Edit them and the grids recompute live.
  7. Providing country (bilateral). For bilateral programmes the funder/providing country is inferred from the funding organisation (IATI participating-org with role = funding) or the reporting organisation — via its ISO registration prefix or a name/agency match — and tagged as inferred. Where it can’t be determined confidently it is left blank, never guessed.
  8. Inflation & PPP. The USD toggle (sidebar) switches nominal vs real constant-2024 USD, deflating each programme by its start-year using US CPI (World Bank, base 2024). PPP adjustment is deliberately omitted: donor budgets are spent on a shifting mix of imported and local goods, so converting them to local purchasing-power terms would imply a precision the data does not support.

Data quality — coverage of this sample

Computed live over the embedded programmes. Each figure is a share of this sample, surfaced so you can judge how complete a given field is before relying on it — not a claim about the full IATI universe.

FX rates — indicative (≈USD per unit), editable

Sample calibration — how representative is this sample?

For each sector: how many programmes are in this embedded sample, the recent IATI universe it is drawn from (live from the official IATI Datastore), the resulting coverage, and — for outside context — recent ODA to that sector from the OECD CRS (latest year, all DAC donors → developing countries). Coverage well under 100% is expected — this is a representative sample, not a census. The OECD column is a different measure (annual ODA US$, a broader all-DAC-donor universe than IATI), shown for scale, not a coverage denominator. Refresh both with datastore_totals.py (free IATI key) and crs_calibration.py (no key).

Exports & data dictionary

Every grid exports to CSV — Programmes and Reported outcomes (filtered to your current view) and the plan basket. The field reference:

FieldWhat it means
USD approxReported amount converted to USD via the indicative fixed FX below; in real mode, deflated to constant-2024 USD by start-year (US CPI). Derived. Blank when no currency/amount (GAP).
BasisWhat the amount represents — budget, commitment, disbursement or expenditure (the last derived from IATI transactions where no budget is reported).
Duration (mo)Months between reported start and end dates. Derived; blank if either date is missing.
Providing countryFor bilaterals, the funder country inferred from the funding/reporting org's ISO registration prefix; blank when not determinable.
Reports resultsWhether the activity publishes any IATI result; indicator-level rows appear in Reported outcomes where available.
Achieved (act/tgt)Indicator actual ÷ target. Derived, shown per row only — never aggregated into a benchmark.
ReachReported beneficiary/indicator count, kept with its label. Non-comparable across activities — deliberately not divided into a cost-per-beneficiary.

Refresh & extend

The dataset is embedded directly in js/data.js — there is no server and no build step; this page is static. To regenerate or grow it: iati_ingest.py holds the shared ingestion core (developing-country + region reference, DAC-sector and stream classification). The per-sector samples are pulled from the official IATI Datastore (free API key) and drawn publisher-capped so no single donor dominates a sector. add_sector.py adds a DAC sector, add_uae.py a publisher; enrich_llm.py adds optional English summaries. Refresh the calibration columns below with datastore_totals.py (Recent IATI; needs the key) and crs_calibration.py (OECD CRS; no key). Set the key as an environment variable — never commit it.
